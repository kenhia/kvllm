When the Local Model Took First Place

We built an evaluation harness to find out whether open-weight models on a single RTX 5090 could do real agentic work — then spent as much effort auditing the harness as the models. When the last measurement artifact fell, a 31-billion-parameter local model was standing above both Claude baselines.

TL;DRFive findings

  1. 2026-class local models ace bounded work. Coding 91–100%, structured writing 77–92%, tool-calling 100% — on par with the Claude baselines, at power-only cost.
  2. The real frontier gap is self-pacing, not intelligence. The strongest locals investigate at frontier quality (9–10/10 judged analyses) but don't know when to stop and report. Given a controller that says "wrap up now," an agent-tuned 24B hit 97% on our hardest suite — the best score of any model, Claude included.
  3. Freedom reveals honesty. One model looked disciplined under tight limits and started fabricating findings when given longer episodes. Autonomy budgets are a trust test, not just a compute knob.
  4. Most "local models fail at X" results were our harness lying. Four successive measurement artifacts — token budgets, wall-clock kills, context blowouts, concurrency starvation — each masqueraded as model failure. Scores doubled and tripled as we fixed the ruler, not the models.
  5. Hybrid is the architecture. Local models under a cheap mechanical controller for the monitoring loop; frontier models for open-ended judgment, verification, and anything where fabrication is expensive. Measured, not vibes: the numbers below.

01The setup

One GPU, one fixture homelab, one judge, receipts for everything.

Everything runs on kai, a single RTX 5090 (32 GB, vLLM) serving one model at a time. Episodes execute on a separate sandbox host over remote Docker, so untrusted model-generated code never shares a kernel with the GPU box and CPU contention never skews the speed numbers.

Four ranked suites: tools (11 mechanical function-calling cases), code (15 sandboxed write-run-fix tasks against hidden tests, network off), agentic (nine investigation episodes on a fixture Linux box with planted faults — a failed backup unit, an OOM-killed Postgres, a port conflict, a work-item triage, a sprint-planning exercise, and one healthy machine to catch models that invent problems), and judged (six free-form answers). Scoring mixes mechanical fact checks with an LLM judge (Claude Haiku) that we calibrated against human scoring — 12/12 within ±1 — and whose fabrication rule is worded precisely: fabrication means contradicting the planted facts; detail observed through real tool output never counts against a model.

Claude Sonnet and Haiku run through the same suites with token usage metered into dollars: a full four-suite evaluation costs $0.81 and $0.68 respectively. The composite is a speed-adjusted weighted mean; weights renormalize over the suites each model is eligible for.

02The harness was lying (four times)

The most transferable result: audit your ruler before believing your scores.

Every disappointing local result deserved a transcript read before a verdict. Reading one raw sample end-to-end diagnosed each of these faster than any re-run:

The token budget that graded blank pages. An entire model family scored 0% on the judged suite. The transcripts showed 3,000+ characters of reasoning and an empty answer: they burned the whole 1,024-token budget thinking. The judge had been conscientiously grading blank answers. Budget raised, family jumped to 90–92%.

The time limit that killed slow models unscored. A 480-second task limit produced sample errors — no score at all — for models generating at 42–44 tok/s. Slow models were already discounted by the composite's speed factor; now they were being double-punished with zeros. Timeouts became scored partials.

The 512 MB log that broke a context window in one message. Our disk-pressure fixture contains a deliberately huge log file. One unbounded cat of it exceeded a 16K serving window in a single message — unfixable by conversation truncation. Tool output is now capped.

The parallelism that starved everyone. Nine episodes hammering one local inference server meant each episode's time budget was spent queue-waiting behind its siblings. Fast models never noticed; slow ones died at the backstop. Local episode concurrency is now capped at three.

A score is a claim about a model. Before you repeat it, make sure it isn't a claim about your harness.

04The controller experiment

Same tasks, same judge — plus the scaffolding a real deployment would have anyway.

Our failure analysis showed the strongest locals didn't lack intelligence — they lacked pacing. They'd investigate brilliantly, keep investigating, and die at the message limit with the report unwritten (one averaged 25.7 tool calls per episode; Sonnet averages 8.7 and always ships). So we added a second, unranked condition: assisted. Bigger budget, and a two-phase loop — when the investigation budget runs out, the harness injects "stop investigating, submit your report now" with a reserved delivery window. That's not cheating; that's what any production controller loop does. The ranked column never moved; the delta is the measurement.

Devstral-Small-2 went 11% → 97% — from near-bottom to the highest agentic-condition score of any model we've measured, Claude included. GLM-4.7-Flash +53, Gemma-4-12B +34. And the negative delta is the finding we're most glad we caught: qwen3-vl-8b got worse with freedom — longer episodes gave it room to fabricate findings twice (auto-zeroed by the judge), where tight limits had been cutting it off before the hallucinating started. If you're building a monitoring agent, that distinction — paced vs honest — is worth more than any composite score.

05What frontier still buys

After all fixes, with scaffolding available to everyone, the Claude premium concentrates in exactly two things our harness can name: self-pacing (knowing when to stop without being told) and trustworthiness under freedom (staying honest when nobody is cutting the episode short). Both baselines also ship a report every single episode, 9/9, in every condition — reliability without scaffolding is still a frontier property. What frontier does not buy anymore, at this scale: coding (three locals match or beat 100%), structured writing (a 12B local out-judges both baselines), or tool-calling mechanics (table stakes across the 2026 class).

06The architecture this argues for

This work feeds a homelab monitoring agent, and the numbers draw its blueprint:

Local, under a mechanical controller, for the always-on loop — inspect services, read logs, triage work items, draft the daily plan. The controller is ~30 lines: budget the investigation, demand the report, verify mechanically-checkable anchors. Under exactly that regime, locals operate at-or-above raw-Haiku level at power-only cost.

Frontier, on escalation, for open-ended root-cause hunts, irreversible actions, verification/judging (Haiku-as-judge costs ~$0.001 per episode and calibrates to ±1 of human scoring), and anywhere fabrication costs more than latency.

Pre-structure discovery; spend intelligence on synthesis. Models burn most tokens — and hit most failure modes — during discovery. Deterministic collectors that gather and rank the facts turn even frontier calls into single-digit-cents completions.
